## Section 1: PM Role, Thinking & Problem Framing (Sessions 1-2)

---

**Q1.** TechFlow is a B2B SaaS startup. The CEO asks three people for their opinion on what to build next. The PM says "we should solve the onboarding drop-off problem," the PO says "we need to groom the sprint backlog for the checkout refactor," and the PMM says "we need to update our competitive positioning before the conference." Who is operating correctly within their role?

- A) Only the PM
- B) Only the PO
- C) Only the PMM
- D) All three are operating within their roles

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**D) All three are operating within their roles.**

- The **PM** is focused on strategic product problems (onboarding drop-off) — correct.
- The **PO** is focused on execution and sprint-level backlog management — correct.
- The **PMM** is focused on external positioning and go-to-market — correct.

Each role has a distinct lane: PM = what & why, PO = delivery execution, PMM = market-facing messaging.

</details>

---

**Q2.** At UrbanCart (a grocery delivery app), the PM proudly reports: "We shipped 14 features this quarter, redesigned the homepage, and pushed 200 commits." The VP of Product is unimpressed. Why?

- A) The PM should have shipped more features
- B) The PM is reporting outputs, not outcomes
- C) The PM should have focused on the homepage only
- D) 200 commits is too few for a quarter

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) The PM is reporting outputs, not outcomes.**

Shipping features and pushing commits are **outputs** (activity). The VP wants to hear **outcomes**: "Onboarding completion improved from 40% to 62%," "Average order value increased by 18%." Good PMs measure impact on user behavior and business metrics, not volume of work shipped.

</details>

---

**Q3.** FitBuddy is a fitness app with declining engagement. The founder says, "Let's add a social feed — Instagram has one and they're huge." What cognitive bias is the founder most likely exhibiting?

- A) Survivorship bias
- B) Anchoring bias
- C) Sunk cost fallacy
- D) Confirmation bias

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**A) Survivorship bias.**

The founder is looking at a **successful** product (Instagram) and copying its feature without considering: (1) the hundreds of apps that added social feeds and failed, (2) whether FitBuddy's users actually want a social feed, and (3) whether the social feed is what makes Instagram successful. Survivorship bias means only studying winners and ignoring the failures.

</details>

**Q4.** A PM at CloudSync (file storage tool) writes this problem statement: "Users need a better drag-and-drop interface." What is wrong with this?

- A) It's too vague
- B) It describes a solution, not a problem
- C) It doesn't mention a metric
- D) Both B and C

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**D) Both B and C.**

"Better drag-and-drop interface" is a **solution** disguised as a problem. A proper problem statement focuses on the user's pain: "Users abandon file uploads 60% of the time because the current upload flow requires 5+ clicks and doesn't show progress." This states the **problem** (upload abandonment), **evidence** (60% drop-off), and **root cause** (too many steps, no feedback) — and includes a metric.

</details>

**Q5.** MediTrack is building a patient appointment app. The PM writes these non-goals: "We will not support video consultations in V1. We will not integrate with insurance billing systems. We will not build a pharmacy module." When should non-goals be defined?

- A) After launch, based on user feedback
- B) At the start, during problem framing and scoping
- C) Only when stakeholders request features outside scope
- D) Non-goals are not a real PM practice

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) At the start, during problem framing and scoping.**

Non-goals should be defined **early** and **explicitly** during the opportunity brief / PRD phase. They prevent scope creep, align stakeholders on what you're NOT doing, and save time by avoiding repeated debates. Writing non-goals is just as important as writing goals.

</details>

**Q6.** A PM at RideEasy (ride-sharing app) applies JTBD thinking. Which of the following is the best JTBD statement?

- A) "Users want a cheaper ride-sharing app"
- B) "When I need to get to the airport early morning, I want a reliable ride booked the night before, so I can avoid the stress of finding transport at 4 AM"
- C) "Users aged 25-35 want ride-sharing for daily commutes"
- D) "Build a scheduled ride feature with fare lock"

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) "When I need to get to the airport early morning, I want a reliable ride booked the night before, so I can avoid the stress of finding transport at 4 AM"**

This follows the JTBD format: **When I** [situation/context], **I want to** [motivation/action], **so I can** [expected outcome]. Option A is too vague. Option C uses demographics (JTBD ignores demographics and focuses on the job/context). Option D is a solution, not a job.

</details>

**Q7.** The PM at EduLearn (online learning platform) writes: "Add gamification badges to courses." How should this be rewritten as an outcome statement?

- A) "Gamification badges will make learners happier"
- B) "Launch badges by Q2"
- C) "Increase course completion rate from 22% to 35% within 90 days"
- D) "Add 10 badge types across all courses"

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C) "Increase course completion rate from 22% to 35% within 90 days."**

An outcome statement follows: **[Direction] + [Metric] + [From X] + [To Y] + [Timeframe]**. Option A has no metric. Option B is a deadline, not an outcome. Option D is an output. Only C describes a measurable change in user behavior.

</details>

**Q8.** LearnHub's PM is framing an opportunity. She gathers: user interview quotes, support ticket data, churn survey results, and competitor feature comparisons. Her manager says she's missing something critical. What is it?

- A) A solution prototype
- B) A "Why Now?" rationale
- C) A Gantt chart
- D) Engineering estimates

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) A "Why Now?" rationale.**

An opportunity brief requires: target segment, problem statement, **why now**, constraints, non-goals, and evidence. "Why Now?" explains why this problem is urgent today — what has changed in the market, technology, or user behavior that makes this the right time. Without it, stakeholders may deprioritize the opportunity.

</details>

## Section 2: Customers, Segments & Competitive Landscape (Sessions 3-4)

**Q9.** ByteBox sells project management software. Their data shows: freelancers sign up a lot but churn in 30 days; mid-size agencies (20-50 people) sign up less but stay for 18+ months and upgrade to paid plans. Who is the ICP?

- A) Freelancers, because they have higher sign-up volume
- B) Mid-size agencies, because they retain and pay
- C) Both equally
- D) Neither — more data is needed

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Mid-size agencies, because they retain and pay.**

ICP (Ideal Customer Profile) is the customer most likely to **buy, stay, and expand**. Freelancers are high volume but high churn with low revenue — they're a poor ICP. Mid-size agencies retain (18+ months) and upgrade (pay more) — that's where the sustainable business is. High sign-ups mean nothing if they all churn.

</details>

**Q10.** HealthPulse is launching a B2B wellness platform for companies. The sales team closes a deal with a large enterprise. The **CHRO** (Chief HR Officer) signed the contract, the **IT team** will deploy it, and **employees** will use it daily. A feature request comes in — from whom should the PM weigh it most carefully?

- A) CHRO only — they signed the check
- B) IT team — they control deployment
- C) Employees — they're the end users
- D) All three, but understand their different needs

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**D) All three, but understand their different needs.**

In B2B, the **buyer** (CHRO), **deployer** (IT), and **user** (employees) are different people with different needs. The CHRO cares about ROI and compliance; IT cares about security and integration; employees care about ease of use. Ignoring any group can kill the deal (buyer), block deployment (IT), or tank adoption (users). A PM must satisfy all stakeholders.

</details>

**Q11.** PetPal is a pet care marketplace. The PM estimates TAM as "all pet owners globally = $300B." The investor says this is meaningless. What should the PM do instead?

- A) Find a bigger TAM number to impress investors
- B) Do a bottom-up estimate: # of target pet owners in target cities × average spend × frequency
- C) Drop market sizing entirely — it's not useful
- D) Use competitor revenue as TAM

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Do a bottom-up estimate.**

A top-down TAM of "$300B all pet owners globally" is useless because PetPal can't serve all pet owners everywhere. A **bottom-up** estimate is more credible: "500K dog owners in our 3 launch cities × $50 avg. monthly spend on grooming/walking × 12 months = $300M SAM. At 5% capture = $15M SOM in Year 1." This is specific, defensible, and actionable.

</details>

**Q12.** NoteGenius is a note-taking app competing with Notion, Evernote, and Google Docs. The PM only analyzes these three direct competitors. What is she missing?

- A) Nothing — three competitors is enough
- B) Alternatives: pen & paper, sticky notes, sending yourself emails, Apple Notes
- C) Complementary products
- D) Investor competitors

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Alternatives: pen & paper, sticky notes, sending yourself emails, Apple Notes.**

**Competitors** are direct products solving the same problem. **Alternatives** are ANY other way users solve the problem today — including non-digital solutions. Often the biggest "competitor" is the user's current hacky workaround (emailing themselves, using pen & paper). If you don't understand alternatives, you don't understand what you're really replacing.

</details>

**Q13.** CookMate (a meal planning app) drafts this positioning statement: "CookMate is an app that helps people cook." What's wrong with it?

- A) It's too short
- B) It lacks target customer, specific need, category, key benefit, and differentiation
- C) It should mention the technology stack
- D) Nothing — simple is better

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) It lacks target customer, specific need, category, key benefit, and differentiation.**

A proper positioning statement follows: "For [target customer] who [statement of need], [product] is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [competitor], our product [primary differentiation]."

Better: "For busy working parents who struggle to plan healthy weeknight meals, CookMate is a meal planning app that generates personalized weekly meal plans with auto-generated grocery lists. Unlike generic recipe apps, CookMate adapts to your family's dietary restrictions and available cooking time."

</details>

**Q14.** SwitchBank is a new digital bank trying to acquire customers from traditional banks. The PM identifies that users have: (1) auto-pay bills linked to their old account, (2) direct deposit set up, (3) 10+ years of transaction history, (4) joint accounts with family members. These collectively represent:

- A) Adoption barriers
- B) Switching costs
- C) Market segmentation factors
- D) Churn indicators

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Switching costs.**

These are all things the user **loses or must redo** when leaving their current bank: migrating auto-pays, changing direct deposit, losing transaction history, re-establishing joint accounts. High switching costs make acquisition harder. SwitchBank should find ways to reduce these — e.g., automatic bill migration, transaction history import, easy joint account setup.

</details>

## Section 3: Research & Analytics (Sessions 5-6)

---

**Q15.** A PM at ShopQuick (e-commerce app) is conducting user interviews. She asks: "Don't you think our checkout is too complicated?" What's wrong with this question?

- A) It's too short
- B) It's a leading question that suggests the answer
- C) It should be multiple choice
- D) Nothing — it's direct and efficient

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) It's a leading question that suggests the answer.**

"Don't you think...too complicated?" suggests the PM's opinion and pushes the user toward agreeing. A better question: "Walk me through the last time you checked out on our app. What was that experience like?" This is **open-ended**, focuses on **past behavior**, and doesn't bias the response.

</details>

---

**Q16.** TravelBee's PM conducts 5 user interviews and collects 47 individual observations. She now needs to find patterns. What technique should she use?

- A) A/B testing
- B) Affinity mapping
- C) Conjoint analysis
- D) Funnel analysis

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Affinity mapping.**

Affinity mapping = taking individual observations/quotes, writing each on a sticky note (or digital equivalent), grouping similar notes into **clusters**, and labeling each cluster with a **theme**. It's the standard technique for synthesizing qualitative research into patterns. A/B testing and funnel analysis are quantitative. Conjoint analysis is for pricing research.

</details>

**Q17.** A PM sends a survey with this question: "How much do you love our new feature? (1=Love it, 5=Absolutely love it)." What's the problem?

- A) The scale should be 1-10
- B) The scale is biased — all options are positive; there's no way to express dissatisfaction
- C) Surveys shouldn't ask about features
- D) The scale is too short

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) The scale is biased — all options are positive; there's no way to express dissatisfaction.**

A 1-5 scale where 1 = "Love it" and 5 = "Absolutely love it" offers no negative option. This forces all respondents into positive responses, making the data meaningless. A proper scale would be balanced: 1 = Very dissatisfied, 3 = Neutral, 5 = Very satisfied.

</details>

**Q18.** DataDash (analytics tool) has this funnel data:

| Step | Users |
|------|-------|
| Visit website | 10,000 |
| Sign up | 3,000 |
| Complete onboarding | 600 |
| Create first dashboard | 180 |
| Active after 7 days | 90 |

Where is the biggest drop-off, and where should the PM focus?

- A) Visit → Sign up (70% drop-off)
- B) Sign up → Complete onboarding (80% drop-off)
- C) Complete onboarding → First dashboard (70% drop-off)
- D) First dashboard → Active after 7 days (50% drop-off)

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Sign up → Complete onboarding (80% drop-off).**

- Visit → Sign up: 10,000 → 3,000 = 70% drop-off
- **Sign up → Complete onboarding: 3,000 → 600 = 80% drop-off** (LARGEST)
- Complete onboarding → First dashboard: 600 → 180 = 70% drop-off
- First dashboard → Active 7 days: 180 → 90 = 50% drop-off

The biggest absolute and percentage drop is at onboarding. Fixing this step has the highest leverage — every improvement here compounds through all downstream steps.

</details>

**Q19.** StreamVibe (a music streaming app) reports: "We have 2 million total sign-ups!" The VP asks, "But are we actually getting better?" What metric would answer this question?

- A) Total sign-ups this month
- B) Cohort retention curves (% of users from each month still active after 30/60/90 days)
- C) Total songs played
- D) App store rating

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Cohort retention curves.**

"Total sign-ups" is a **vanity metric** — it only goes up and tells you nothing about product quality. **Cohort retention** groups users by sign-up month and tracks what % are still active over time. If January's cohort retains at 30% but March's retains at 45%, the product is improving. This answers "are we getting better?" directly.

</details>

**Q20.** QuizMaster's PM wants to understand how satisfied users are with a specific new feature (quiz sharing). Which metric is most appropriate?

- A) NPS
- B) CSAT
- C) DAU/MAU ratio
- D) Churn rate

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) CSAT (Customer Satisfaction Score).**

**CSAT** measures satisfaction with a **specific interaction or feature** ("How satisfied are you with quiz sharing?"). **NPS** measures overall brand loyalty/recommendation intent — it's too broad for a single feature. DAU/MAU and churn rate are engagement metrics, not satisfaction metrics.

</details>

**Q21.** A food delivery app, BiteNow, is choosing its North Star Metric. Which is the best choice?

- A) Number of app downloads
- B) Number of orders delivered per month
- C) Total revenue
- D) Number of restaurants on the platform

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Number of orders delivered per month.**

A good NSM must: (1) reflect **value delivered to the user** — each order = a meal delivered, (2) be a **leading indicator of revenue** — more orders = more revenue, (3) be **actionable** — teams can improve conversion, delivery speed, restaurant selection. Downloads (A) is vanity. Revenue (C) is a lagging indicator. Restaurant count (D) is a supply metric, not a value metric.

</details>

**Q22.** FinTrack (personal finance app) sets this OKR:

**Objective:** Make budgeting effortless for millennials.
**KR1:** Launch budget alerts feature by March 15.
**KR2:** Ship iOS and Android versions.
**KR3:** Write 5 blog posts about budgeting.

What's wrong with these Key Results?

- A) There are too few KRs
- B) The KRs are tasks/outputs, not measurable outcomes
- C) The Objective is too ambitious
- D) KRs should be annual, not quarterly

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) The KRs are tasks/outputs, not measurable outcomes.**

"Launch feature," "Ship versions," and "Write blog posts" are **activities/outputs** — they describe what you'll do, not what impact it will have. Better KRs: "Reduce avg. time to create a budget from 12 min to 3 min," "Increase monthly active budgeters from 5K to 20K," "Achieve 60% activation rate for new budget tool users." KRs must be **outcomes with numbers**.

</details>

**Q23.** BrightLearn (e-learning platform) runs an A/B test. After 2 days (with 200 users per group), the variant shows a 15% improvement in sign-ups with p=0.03. The PM wants to ship it immediately. What's the risk?

- A) No risk — p < 0.05 means it's significant
- B) The sample size is too small and 2 days doesn't capture weekly patterns — the result may be a false positive
- C) A/B tests should never run for more than 2 days
- D) p=0.03 means the test failed

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) The sample size is too small and 2 days doesn't capture weekly patterns.**

Even though p < 0.05, **2 days with only 200 users per group** is almost certainly too small a sample and too short a duration. Behavior varies by day of week (weekday vs weekend). The apparent 15% improvement could be noise, a novelty effect, or day-of-week skew. Best practice: run A/B tests for **at least 1-2 full weeks** with a proper sample size calculation.

</details>

**Q24.** GigHub (freelancer marketplace) has this metrics tree:

```
NSM: Monthly completed gigs
```

Which is the best guardrail metric?

- C) Number of marketing emails sent
- D) Office headcount

A **guardrail** is a metric that must NOT degrade while you optimize the primary metric. **Q25.** A PM writes this A/B test hypothesis: "If we change the button color to green, more people will click it." What's missing?

- A) Nothing — it's clear
- B) The "because" (reasoning), the specific metric, and the expected effect size
- C) The button's location
- D) The hex code of the green

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) The "because" (reasoning), specific metric, and expected effect size.**

A proper hypothesis: "We believe that changing the CTA button from gray to green will increase checkout click-through rate by 10% because the green color creates higher visual contrast and signals a positive action." This includes: **what** you're changing, **which metric** you expect to move, **by how much**, and **why** you believe this. Without these, you can't design a proper experiment or determine sample size.

</details>

## Section 5: Strategy, Business Case & Unit Economics (Sessions 8-9)

**Q26.** CloudHR (HR SaaS) has the following unit economics:
- Average Revenue Per Customer: $500/month
- Gross Margin: 80%
- Average Customer Lifetime: 24 months
- Customer Acquisition Cost: $6,000

What is the LTV:CAC ratio, and is the business healthy?

- A) 16:1 — extremely healthy
- B) 3.2:1 — healthy
- C) 1.6:1 — unhealthy, CAC is too high
- D) 0.8:1 — the company is losing money on every customer

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C) 1.6:1 — unhealthy, CAC is too high.**

LTV = ARPU × Gross Margin × Lifetime = $500 × 0.80 × 24 = **$9,600**
LTV:CAC = $9,600 / $6,000 = **1.6:1**

A healthy ratio is **> 3:1**. At 1.6:1, CloudHR spends too much to acquire customers relative to the value they extract. They need to either **reduce CAC** (cheaper acquisition channels), **increase ARPU** (upsell/pricing), **improve retention** (longer lifetime), or **improve margins**.

</details>

**Q27.** SnackBox (subscription snack delivery) data:
- CAC: $40
- Monthly subscription: $30
- Gross margin: 50%
- Average customer stays: 8 months

What is the payback period?

- A) 1.3 months
- B) 2.7 months
- C) 4 months
- D) 8 months

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) 2.7 months.**

Monthly Gross Profit = $30 × 50% = **$15/month**
Payback Period = CAC / Monthly Gross Profit = $40 / $15 = **2.67 months ≈ 2.7 months**

This is healthy — the benchmark is payback **under 12 months**. SnackBox recovers its acquisition cost in less than 3 months, leaving ~5 months of pure profit per customer.

</details>

**Q28.** A startup, AutoPilot, has spent $2M and 18 months building an AI scheduling assistant. Early metrics show: 5% activation, NPS of -20, and 90% churn in 30 days. The CEO says, "We've invested too much to stop now." What bias is the CEO exhibiting?

- A) Confirmation bias
- B) Anchoring bias
- C) Sunk cost fallacy
- D) Survivorship bias

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C) Sunk cost fallacy.**

The CEO is continuing to invest because of **past investment** ($2M, 18 months), not future potential. The metrics are catastrophic: 5% activation, negative NPS, 90% churn. Rational decision-making evaluates **future value only** — the $2M is gone regardless. This is exactly why **kill criteria** should be set before building: "If activation < 10% after 90 days, we stop."

</details>

Marketplace (C) implies they connect buyers and sellers without controlling supply. Freemium (D) doesn't fit physical goods.

**Q31.** VidStream (video streaming) offers 3 tiers: Free (ads, SD), Pro ($9.99, no ads, HD), Premium ($14.99, no ads, 4K, offline). 80% of users are on Free and never upgrade. What's the most likely problem?

- A) The free tier is too generous — users have no reason to upgrade
- B) The pricing is too expensive
- C) They need a 4th tier
- D) Video streaming can't be monetized

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**A) The free tier is too generous — users have no reason to upgrade.**

If 80% of users never upgrade, the free tier likely satisfies their needs sufficiently. This is a classic **freemium packaging mistake** — the free tier should give enough value to hook users but create enough friction (ads, SD quality, limited content) to motivate upgrading. VidStream should tighten the free tier or add features that create stronger upgrade incentives.

</details>

**Q32.** A PM at WellnessAI wants to test if users will pay for AI-generated personalized workout plans. The riskiest assumption is desirability (do users even want this?). Budget is very limited. What's the fastest, cheapest test?

- A) Build the full AI model and launch
- B) Concierge MVP — manually create workout plans for 10 users
- C) Fake-door test — create a landing page with "Get Your AI Plan" button and measure clicks
- D) Build a mobile app prototype in Figma

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C) Fake-door test.**

The riskiest assumption is **desirability** (demand). A fake-door/smoke test is the **fastest and cheapest** way to test demand: create a landing page describing the AI workout plan, add a "Get Early Access" CTA, drive traffic, and measure click-through rate. If < 2% click, demand is low. Concierge MVP (B) is good but slower and tests the value prop, not raw demand. Building the AI (A) is extremely expensive and premature.

</details>

**Q33.** BookClub (a reading community app) has identified these assumptions:
1. Readers want to discuss books with strangers (desirability)
2. We can build real-time group chat at scale (feasibility)
3. Users will pay $5/month for premium discussion groups (viability)

Which assumption should they test FIRST?

- A) #2 — Feasibility, because it's technically challenging
- B) #3 — Viability, because revenue matters most
- C) #1 — Desirability, because if nobody wants it, nothing else matters
- D) Test all three simultaneously

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**C) #1 — Desirability, because if nobody wants it, nothing else matters.**

Always test **desirability first**. If users don't want to discuss books with strangers, it doesn't matter if you can build real-time chat or charge $5/month. The hierarchy: Desirability → Feasibility → Viability. Don't invest engineering effort (feasibility) or pricing research (viability) until you've confirmed someone actually wants this.

</details>

**Q34.** LegalBot wants to test if small law firms will use an AI contract reviewer. They have no product yet. They plan to have a lawyer manually review contracts behind the scenes while the user thinks it's AI. What MVP type is this?

- A) Concierge MVP
- B) Wizard of Oz MVP
- C) Fake-door test
- D) Prototype

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Wizard of Oz MVP.**

In a **Wizard of Oz** MVP, the product **appears automated** to the user, but a **human operates it behind the scenes**. The user thinks AI is reviewing contracts, but it's actually a lawyer. This tests the **user experience and value proposition** without building the technology. A **Concierge MVP** is different — the user knows a human is providing the service (no deception about the mechanism).

</details>

**Q35.** StyleBox (fashion subscription) generates 20 solution ideas for "help users discover clothes matching their style." The PM immediately picks the top 3 she likes best. What's the problem?

- A) 20 ideas is too many
- B) She should score ideas against assumptions (desirability, feasibility, viability) and risk-rank, not pick based on personal preference
- C) She should build all 20
- D) She should let engineering pick

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) She should score ideas against assumptions and risk-rank, not pick based on personal preference.**

Personal preference is not a prioritization framework. The PM should: (1) map each idea's **assumptions** across desirability, feasibility, and viability, (2) **rank by risk** — which assumptions are most uncertain, (3) pick the ideas where testing is cheapest and the potential impact is highest. This is the **assumption mapping** process.

</details>

**CS1-Q1.** Calculate FreshKart's unit economics: LTV and LTV:CAC ratio. Is the business sustainable?

- C) LTV = Rs. 624; LTV:CAC = 1.2:1 — unsustainable
- D) LTV = Rs. 3,120; LTV:CAC = 6.2:1 — healthy

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

- Lifetime = 6 months
- **LTV = 104 × 6 = Rs. 624**
- **LTV:CAC = 624/500 = 1.25:1**

This is **unsustainable**. Healthy is > 3:1. FreshKart barely recovers its acquisition cost. They need to: increase AOV, increase order frequency, reduce delivery costs, improve retention (reduce 15% monthly churn), or reduce CAC.

This directly addresses the #1 churn reason and makes the product's value tangible.

A streak counter (A) is a gamification tactic, not progress tracking — it shows consistency, not improvement.

</details>

**CS5-Q3.** Top feature request: "Connect with a real therapist." BetterHelp already does this. Should WellMind build therapist matching?

- A) Yes — users are asking for it
- B) Be cautious: therapist matching is an entirely different business (regulatory requirements, therapist recruitment, licensing, liability, quality control). It may be better to partner with an existing platform rather than build from scratch. Evaluate build vs. partner vs. refer.
- C) No — never build what users ask for
- D) Acquire BetterHelp

**B) Be cautious — evaluate build vs. partner vs. refer.**

Therapist matching is a fundamentally different business: (1) **Regulatory complexity** — therapist licensing varies by state/country, (2) **Supply acquisition** — recruiting and vetting therapists, (3) **Liability** — clinical outcomes, (4) **Quality control** — ensuring therapeutic standards. WellMind's core competency is technology-driven self-help, not clinical services.

Options: (1) **Build** — expensive, risky, 18+ month effort, (2) **Partner** — integrate with BetterHelp/Talkspace via API (get referral revenue), (3) **Refer** — provide curated directory with one-click booking. The PM should evaluate which option delivers user value fastest with least risk.

**CS5-Q4.** WellMind's D7 retention is 35% and D30 retention is 15%. Power user analysis shows that users who complete a "mood check-in" on 3+ days in the first week have 4x better D30 retention. The PM wants to improve retention. Best approach?

- A) Make mood check-ins mandatory — require them before accessing any content
- B) Design the onboarding to naturally guide users toward 3 mood check-ins in the first week: Day 1 welcome with first check-in, Day 2-3 reminder notifications ("How are you feeling today?"), in-app prompts after meditation sessions, and a "7-day baseline" challenge. Nudge, don't force.
- C) Remove mood check-ins — they're extra friction
- D) Email users monthly about mood check-ins

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Design onboarding to naturally guide toward 3 mood check-ins in the first week.**

The data suggests mood check-ins are correlated with (and likely drive) retention — they create a personal data investment and habit loop. But **forcing** check-ins creates friction and may push users away. The PM should: (1) Include first mood check-in in onboarding flow, (2) Send gentle push notifications on Days 2, 3, 5 ("Take 30 seconds — how are you feeling?"), (3) Show evolving mood chart after 3+ check-ins ("Your baseline is forming!"), (4) Celebrate the milestone ("3 check-ins complete — your mood report is ready!"). This is **nudge architecture** — designing the experience to guide behavior without removing choice.

</details>

**CS5-Q5.** WellMind's CAC is $8 with 60% organic acquisition. The PM wants to scale to 500K MAU (from 80K). Which growth strategy is most efficient?

- A) 10x the paid advertising budget
- B) Invest in the organic growth engine: content marketing (blog posts on mental health tips → SEO), social proof (user testimonials, app store ratings), referral program ("Give a friend 1 free month"), and content/SEO loop (user-generated content like shared meditation playlists or journaling prompts)
- C) Billboard advertising
- D) Reduce the price to $1/month

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B) Invest in the organic growth engine.**

60% organic acquisition at $8 CAC is already efficient. To scale 6x, **doubling down on organic** is more sustainable than 10x paid spend (which would increase CAC due to market saturation). Strategy: (1) **Content marketing** — mental health articles ranking in Google (content/SEO loop), (2) **App store optimization** — ratings, reviews, screenshots, (3) **Referral program** — leverage high NPS (+55 premium) for word-of-mouth, (4) **Social sharing** — shareable meditation stats, (5) **Community** — forums/groups that create content and attract search traffic.

Paid (A) is a linear growth model (spend more → get proportionally more, with diminishing returns). Organic/referral is a **compounding** growth model.

</details>

# QUICK REVISION: 10 THINGS MOST LIKELY TO APPEAR IN YOUR EXAM

1. **Outputs vs Outcomes** — Always pick the outcome (behavior change + metric), not the output (feature shipped)
2. **LTV:CAC calculation** — Know the formula cold. Healthy = > 3:1
3. **Correlation ≠ Causation** — Just because two metrics move together doesn't mean one causes the other. Always consider self-selection bias.
4. **Funnel analysis** — Find the biggest drop-off. Fix the top of the funnel first.
5. **A/B testing pitfalls** — Small sample, short duration, peeking, practical vs statistical significance
6. **RICE prioritization** — (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort
7. **Kano model** — Must-be → Performance → Delighter. Always fulfill Must-be first.
8. **North Star Metric** — Must reflect core value delivered to users, be a leading indicator of revenue, and be actionable
9. **B2B: Buyer ≠ User** — Satisfy both. If users hate the product, buyers won't renew.
10. **Kill criteria before you build** — Prevents sunk cost fallacy. Specific, measurable, time-bound.
